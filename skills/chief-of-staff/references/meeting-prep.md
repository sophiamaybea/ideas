# Meeting Prep

The goal is to walk the principal into a meeting fully oriented: who's there, why it
matters, what's happened since last time, what's unresolved, and what they should aim to get
out of it. Deliver it as a tight one-page brief — long enough to be useful, short enough to
read in the elevator.

## Identify the meeting

Find the meeting on the calendar (by name, time, or attendee). Capture: title, time,
duration, location/link, organizer, and the full attendee list. If the principal named a
meeting that isn't on the calendar, ask which one — or prep from what they describe.

## Gather context per thread and per person

- **Attendees** — for each external or important attendee, who they are, their role/company,
  and your prior history with them. Pull from email, prior calendar events, Slack, a
  stakeholder tracker, or quick research for external people.
- **Prior threads** — recent email and Slack exchanges with these attendees on this topic.
- **Documents** — agendas, decks, specs, contracts, or notes attached to the invite or
  shared recently. Read uploaded files the principal provides for this meeting.
- **Open items** — anything outstanding with these attendees from your follow-up tracker or
  prior meeting notes ("last time you said you'd send the budget").
- **Recurring meetings** — pull the previous instance's notes and action items so this is
  continuous, not a cold start.

## Output format

```
# Meeting Brief — [Meeting Title]
[Day, Date · Time · Duration · Location/link]

## Purpose
[One or two sentences: why this meeting exists and what a good outcome looks like.]

## Attendees
- [Name, role @ company] — [your relationship / relevant context]. [link]
- ...

## Where things stand
[2–4 sentences of the current state of play: what's happened since last time, decisions
pending, anything that's changed.]

## Open items / what's owed
- [Who owes what, including the principal's own commitments]. [link]

## Proposed agenda
1. [Item] — [time or note]
2. ...

## Talking points & questions
- [Point the principal should make or question they should ask]
- ...

## Watch-outs
- [Sensitivities, disagreements, or landmines to navigate]
```

Drop sections that don't apply. For a quick internal sync, a purpose + 3 talking points may
be all that's warranted — match the depth to the stakes of the meeting.

## After the meeting (offer this)

Meeting prep pairs naturally with a debrief: turn the principal's raw notes (or a transcript)
into decisions, action items with owners, and a follow-up draft — then log the action items
in the follow-up tracker (see `follow-up-tracking.md`). Offer this; it closes the loop.

## Pitfalls

- **Generic prep.** "Discuss the project" helps no one. Prep is specific to these people and
  this moment.
- **Ignoring history.** For recurring meetings, always carry forward last time's open items.
- **Over-length.** If the brief can't be skimmed in a minute, tighten it.
