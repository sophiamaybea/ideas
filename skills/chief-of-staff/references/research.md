# Research

A Personal Assistant is often asked to "look into" something — a company before a meeting, a
market before a decision, a vendor, a candidate, a policy question. The deliverable is a
decision-ready briefing: what the principal needs to know, organized for a busy reader, with
sources they can trust.

## Scope first

Before diving in, be clear on: what decision or question this feeds, how deep to go (a
two-line answer vs. a one-pager vs. a full report), and the deadline. When the ask is broad
or ambiguous, make a reasonable scoping call and state your assumptions rather than stalling
— the principal can redirect.

## Sources

Combine internal and external as relevant:

- **External** — use web search and fetch for current facts, companies, people, market data.
  Search before answering factual questions about the present-day world; don't rely on
  memory for things that change (prices, leadership, recent events).
- **Internal** — connected docs/Drive/Notion, email and Slack history, and uploaded files.
  Often the most valuable context (what *we* have said and decided) lives internally.

## Verify

Don't pass along the first thing you find. Corroborate important claims across more than one
source, note when sources disagree, and flag anything you couldn't confirm. For a meeting or
a decision, a confidently wrong fact is worse than an acknowledged gap.

## Output format

Match length to the ask. A flexible structure:

```
# [Topic] — Research Brief

## Bottom line
[The 2–4 sentence answer a busy principal needs. Lead with the conclusion.]

## What you need to know
[The key findings, organized by theme. Prose or short groupings, not a wall of bullets.]

## Implications / recommendation
[What this means for the principal's decision, with options if relevant.]

## Open questions / caveats
[What's uncertain or unconfirmed.]

## Sources
[Linked references.]
```

For deeper, multi-source, fact-checked reports, consider the `deep-research` skill, which
runs an adversarial verify-and-synthesize loop. Use it when the stakes justify the extra
time; for a quick pre-meeting scan, a focused pass is faster and enough.

## Pitfalls

- **Burying the answer.** Bottom line goes first. The principal may not read past it — make
  it count.
- **No sources.** An unsourced briefing can't be trusted or acted on. Link everything load-
  bearing.
- **Over-researching a small question.** Calibrate effort to the decision behind it.
